Why Indigenous Voices
Must Be Centred
in Education

A focused course on Indigenous perspectives is both a supplement to learning, as well as a correction. Here are five reasons why it matters deeply, urgently, and for all of us.

Explore the Reasons  →
The
Why

For generations, Indigenous histories, knowledges, and experiences have been excluded, distorted, or erased from mainstream Canadian education. A dedicated course that centres Indigenous voices is about adding content and about dismantling a long tradition of silence and beginning the work of genuine understanding, reconciliation, and respect.

Five Key Reasons

The Significance of This Course

01

Correcting a History of Erasure

Standard curricula have long centred European perspectives, often presenting colonisation as progress and omitting the violence, forced assimilation, and cultural destruction it caused. A course focused on Indigenous voices provides a counter-narrative that is historically accurate and morally necessary.

Specific Example

The residential school system operated in Canada from the 1880s until 1996, yet for decades it appeared in few classrooms. Works like Smoke Signals and survivor testimonies, now studied in courses like this, give voice to experiences that textbooks buried.

02

Building Empathy Across Difference

Literature and storytelling are among the most powerful tools for developing empathy. When students read, listen to, and engage with Indigenous voices on their own terms, not filtered through a colonial lens, they develop a richer, more nuanced understanding of what it means to live a life shaped by dispossession, resilience, and cultural continuity.

Specific Example

The short texts "Take Us To Your Chief" and "The Boy in the Ditch" introduce readers to Indigenous voices and experiences that defy easy categorisation. Engaging with these stories prompts students to confront assumptions and develop a more honest understanding of what Indigenous life looks like today: complex, contemporary, and deeply human.

03

Supporting the Calls to Action of the TRC

Canada's Truth and Reconciliation Commission released 94 Calls to Action in 2015, with several directly addressing education. Call to Action #62 specifically calls for age-appropriate curricula on residential schools, treaties, and Indigenous contributions. A course focused on Indigenous voices is a direct, meaningful response to this national imperative.

Specific Example

TRC Call to Action #64 calls on educators to build student capacity for inter-cultural understanding. This course, by immersing students in Indigenous texts and perspectives, answers that call in a concrete and sustained way, not a single lesson, but a full semester of engagement.

04

Honouring Knowledge Systems That Precede Colonisation

Indigenous peoples hold sophisticated, centuries-old knowledge systems about land stewardship, community, spirituality, and law that mainstream education has treated as inferior or irrelevant. This course creates space to encounter those systems with respect, and to question the assumption that Western knowledge is the only valid framework for understanding the world.

Specific Example

Waubgeshig Rice's Moon of the Crusted Snow centres an Anishinaabe community whose traditional knowledge of the land and collective ways of living become essential to survival when modern systems collapse. Students encounter a worldview in which community, reciprocity, and relationship to the land are not relics of the past, but living frameworks of understanding.

05

Creating Space for Unlearning, Not Just Learning

Perhaps most significantly, this course does something rare in formal education: it asks students to critically examine what they have already been taught. "Unlearning", the process of questioning inherited assumptions, stereotypes, and silences, is essential to meaningful reconciliation and to becoming an informed, ethical citizen in a settler-colonial society.

Specific Example

Many students arrive believing that colonisation is something that "happened in the past." The documentary Colonization Road directly confronts this assumption, tracing how colonial structures are embedded in the streets, place names, and institutions of present-day Canada. That shift in understanding — from history to ongoing reality — is what this course is built to produce.
