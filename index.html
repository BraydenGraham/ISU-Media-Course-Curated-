Why Indigenous Voices
Must Be Central
in Education

A course that focuses on Indigenous perspectives is not just an addition to learning — it is also a correction. Here are five reasons why it matters deeply, urgently, and for all of us.

Explore the Reasons  →
The
Why

For generations, Indigenous histories, knowledge, and experiences have been left out, twisted, or erased from mainstream Canadian education. A course that focuses on Indigenous voices does more than add new content. It works to break down a long tradition of silence and begins the work of real understanding, reconciliation, and respect.

Five Key Reasons

The Importance of This Course

01

Correcting a History That Left Voices Out

For a long time, school lessons have focused mainly on European points of view. They often described colonization as progress and left out the violence, forced assimilation, and cultural destruction it caused. A course that focuses on Indigenous voices tells a different side of the story — one that is both historically true and morally important.

Specific Example

The residential school system ran in Canada from the 1880s until 1996, but for decades, it was barely taught in schools. Works like Smoke Signals and the stories of survivors, now studied in courses like this, share experiences that textbooks once hid.

02

Building Empathy for People Who Are Different

Stories and literature are some of the most powerful tools for building empathy. When students read, listen to, and connect with Indigenous voices in their own words, not changed by a colonial point of view, they gain a deeper, more detailed understanding of what it means to live a life shaped by loss of land, strength, and the survival of culture.

Specific Example

The short stories "Take Us To Your Chief" and "The Boy in the Ditch" introduce readers to Indigenous voices and experiences that don't fit into simple boxes. Reading these stories pushes students to question their assumptions and gain a more honest understanding of what Indigenous life looks like today: complex, modern, and deeply human.

03

Supporting the Calls to Action of the TRC

Canada's Truth and Reconciliation Commission released 94 Calls to Action in 2015, and several of them are about education. Call to Action #62 asks for age-appropriate lessons on residential schools, treaties, and Indigenous contributions. A course that focuses on Indigenous voices is a direct, meaningful response to this important national goal.

Specific Example

TRC Call to Action #64 asks teachers to help students understand other cultures better. This course does that by deeply involving students in Indigenous texts and perspectives. It answers that call in a real and lasting way — not just one lesson, but a full semester of learning.

04

Honouring Knowledge Systems That Came Before Colonization

Indigenous peoples hold advanced, centuries-old knowledge systems about caring for the land, community, spirituality, and law. Mainstream education has often treated this knowledge as less important or not relevant. This course makes space to learn about these systems with respect, and to question the idea that Western knowledge is the only correct way to understand the world.

Specific Example

Waubgeshig Rice's Moon of the Crusted Snow focuses on an Anishinaabe community. Their traditional knowledge of the land and shared ways of living become key to survival when modern systems fail. Students see a way of thinking in which community, helping one another, and connection to the land are not just things from the past, but living ways of understanding the world today.

05

Creating Space for Unlearning, Not Just Learning

Most importantly, this course does something rare in school: it asks students to carefully question what they have already been taught. "Unlearning" means questioning the assumptions, stereotypes, and silences we grew up with. This process is key to real reconciliation and to becoming an informed, ethical citizen in a society shaped by colonization.

Specific Example

Many students come into this course believing that colonization is something that "happened in the past." The documentary Colonization Road directly challenges this idea. It shows how colonial structures are built into the streets, place names, and institutions of Canada today. That shift in understanding — from seeing colonization as history to seeing it as an ongoing reality — is exactly what this course aims to create.
